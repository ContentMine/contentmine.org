How it works

Perhaps you have a literature review to do? Or reviewing articles for a clinical trial, or another criteria? Or trying to keep up to date with the stream of publications in your field? Great! You'll be able to find those key papers out of stacks of thousands, with minimal effort. Every day we retrieve thousands of newly published academic articles, then we extract and share factual statements about the content of each article through our API (coming soon).

The software stack running in the background provides tools for getting papers from many online sources, normalising them, then processing them to lookup and/or search for key terms, phrases, patterns, statements, and more The stream of facts will be made available in a standardized, easy-to-reuse way through an open API (Application Programming Interface).

For some inspiration, have a look at our work extracting thousands of phylogenetic trees from the literature, our video showing discovery of research papers relevant to the zika virus outbreak, or our presentation about using content mining in clinical trials reviews.

We would also love to talk to you about any particular use cases you have in mind, and we'll help you understand how to use the API to meet your requirements. So contact us any time.

Eventually, we aim to provide ways for you to customise our daily content mining tasks, and collaboratively manage facts of interest to you. This means that you could more easily find articles relevant to your work, and also discover more relevant research by exploring previously unseen correlations. Check back here regularly for more info, coming soon.

Events / workshops / training

We can provide workshops and training sessions of various forms, either as standalone events of half a day up to two days, or as sessions in part of a larger event such as a conference. We can customise the session to your needs or to a particular research topic, or can provide a general introduction to content mining, our tools and software, the copyright law and UK exceptions to it, and more. We often attend events throughout the year, and also host our own on occasion.

Our project blog details the previous and upcoming events that we have attended and/or delivered.

Our software repository on github contains all of our previous workshop materials.

If you are interested in hiring us to run an event for you, or having us attend an event, please do get in touch with us - we would be very happy to discuss the possibilities.

Software / download / contribute

The main way to access our tools is via the API, but all our software is freely available with an open source licence on our github repository.

Start by following our installation instructions, then work through some of our tutorials.

These are work in progress, so if you find issues with the software or with the docs/tutorials, please do create issues on github for us to fix - each repo has an issue tracker, so for example if you have an issue with the AMI software go to https://github.com/ContentMine/ami/issues.

You can also suggest improvements and further developments via the issue tracker, and if you'd like to contribute please either comment on an issue you'd like to solve, or get in touch to discuss how else you could help - not just with coding either, we'd be happy to hear from academics with new ideas too!

The ContentMine team

ContentMine began when Peter Murray-Rust, a scientist and advocate for open science, sought to use a UK copyright exception to mine the scientific literature as a form of new research.

The Shuttleworth Foundation believed that Peter could change the world, so they funded him since 2014 as a Shuttleworth Fellow to lead the ContentMine project, to demonstrate the usefulness of content mining and to provide tools and services to others.

Content mining (also called text and data mining) means processing information in search of new information. To start content mining, some source material is required. Any digital document would do, but automated processing of documents is often restricted by copyright. However in 2014 the UK introduced the Hargreaves exception, stipulating that anyone in the UK with the right to read a document also has the right to mine it, overriding any copyright / jurisdictional restrictions. So Peter put together a team of people to take advantage of this, and to develop tools and software that could extract uncopyrightable factual data from research articles and share it without restriction to the world.

ContentMine is now a company directed by Peter, Jenny Molloy (a scientist and project manager), Karien Bezuidenhout (a Shuttleworth representative), and Mark MacGillivray (founder of Cottage Labs). The company is supported by a wider team of staff and volunteers including Yvonne Nobis (operations), Ross Mounce (academic outreach), Richard Smith-Unna (academic and software development), Tom Arrow (software development), Charles Oppenheim (academic publishing expert), Chris Kittel (training manager and data visualisation), Stefan Kasberger (training and research community manager). And we also have a world class advisory board consisting of Laura James, Joe McArthur, John Wilbanks, Puneet Kishor, Heather Joseph, Cameron Neylon and Erin McKiernan.
